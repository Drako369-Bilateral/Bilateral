Bilateral

A project on ambidexterity. Two distinct mechanisms — additive and subtractive — for two ontologies of the body, applied across theatre, medicine, combat, and play.

Additive mechanism

Build

Bilateral competence formed through repeated training and Hebbian plasticity — the path that makes a musician or athlete ambidextrous.

Subtractive mechanism

Release

A latent bilateral symmetry, made available by reducing chronic co-contraction — the path observed in Taiji practice.

Grounded in doctoral research

One theoretical core behind every field

The project rests on a experimental thesis in theoretical philosophy, integrating Merleau-Ponty's body schema, Varela's neurophenomenology, and Sainburg's dynamic dominance hypothesis. Its key claim: the subtractive mechanism does not abolish lateralization — it makes it elective. Four thematic clusters carry the idea outward.

About

A question lived before it was studied

Bilateral grew out of an experimental thesis in theoretical philosophy and a first-person experience: becoming bilateralized, from right-lateralization, through Yang-style Taiji (Patrick Kelly lineage).

Not an isolated anecdote, but the starting point for a broader question about what makes a body bilaterally competent — and what that says about mind, body, and freedom of action.
