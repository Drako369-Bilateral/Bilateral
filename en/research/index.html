The research

Research built so it can fail.

Bilateral Education comes from an experimental thesis in philosophy, not from a gym. Its hypotheses are declared and falsifiable: they say precisely where they could be wrong. Here you’ll find the theory, the interdisciplinary framework and the sources — for anyone who wants to test it or take it further.

Download the documents DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21321170

The thesis in one sentence

The subtractive doesn’t abolish lateralization: it makes it elective.

The body reaches ambidexterity in two ways. The additive one builds the missing competence through repetition. The subtractive one adds nothing: it frees a symmetry already present, by reducing the chronic contraction that kept a capable side still. Dominance stops being a suffered fate and becomes a choice.

Additive

Build

Add the missing competence, through repetition and plasticity.

Subtractive

Release

Reduce the chronic contraction that held back an already-capable side.

The more recent work adds a clarification that corrects a point of the thesis: neither mechanism presupposes the other. Both presuppose connection — the body’s ability to transmit load from one side to the other. That’s why the first examination isn’t of laterality, but of connection.

In summary

Seven results.

  1. Interhemispheric inhibition is not a state, but an operation. An instant before every movement the brain performs a switch — the gate: it releases the brake on one side, keeps it on the other.
  2. The gate is asymmetric. In right-handers pre-movement disinhibition appears before the dominant hand, and not before the non-dominant one. The weak side isn’t weak: it’s a side to which release is never granted.
  3. The gate is not the mode. The gate is a discrete event (40-150 ms); the mode is a sustained state — a guard stance — that the current paradigm, measuring performance rather than initiation, doesn’t look at.
  4. The mode isn’t a side: it’s a diagonal. A crossed chain (left leg → trunk → right shoulder). Roles, not conditions.
  5. Tone has three states. Stiffness (necessary), connecting tension (structure), orphan tone (to be set down). Only the third is the target of the subtractive mechanism.
  6. The cycle breaks at two opposite points. The slack can’t load; the anchored can’t set down. The rebound is the observable marker of the failed switch.
  7. Elective lateralization isn’t an acquired state, but a sustained operation. It doesn’t become automatic: it’s exercised, like lucidity.

The framework

Three fields, one problem.

Philosophy of mind

Merleau-Ponty (intentional arc, body schema), Gallagher (schema vs body image), Varela (neurophenomenology). The subtractive reads as a retuning of the intentional arc: the way the body reaches toward the world.

Neuroscience

Interhemispheric inhibition and its asymmetry (Ferbert 1992, Duque 2007, Liuzzi 2010-2011): the pre-movement gate that opens for one side and not the other. Plasticity is the common substrate of building and un-learning a tension.

Movement science

Sainburg’s dynamic dominance: the preferred side is the one the nervous system chooses each time, not the only one able to act. It’s the experimental basis of elective lateralization.

Falsifiability

How to disprove it.

A thesis that can’t lose is worth nothing. Here are some distinct, falsifiable predictions, with an indication of how a lab could test them.

  • Gain without strength. After subtractive practice the non-dominant side improves without any rise in maximal strength or muscle cross-section, with reduced antagonist co-contraction, measurable by EMG.
  • Less interhemispheric inhibition. TMS measures that decrease after subtractive training — an effect not required by the additive one.
  • Kinematic signature. Less «braked» trajectories on the non-dominant side: lower jerk, earlier movement onset.
  • Lateralization preserved. Dominance isn’t abolished: it returns on demand. It’s the test of the «elective» clause, which distinguishes the method from mere homogenisation.
  • The decisive experiment: perception vs instruction. Two groups, same exercise. The first gets external instruction («more fluid, wider»), the second perceptual instruction («feel where the weight loads, feel when it stops»). Prediction: only the second develops elective lateralization. If they converge, the constitutive role of awareness is false, and this is just another motor-learning method.

The rebound — the trajectory that returns instead of passing — is the observable marker the whole protocol rests on.
