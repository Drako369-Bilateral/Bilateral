Postural training: how to actually improve your posture

Jacopo Fedeli · 8 min read

In short. Postural training helps, but its results last exactly as long as your attention: the moment you stop thinking about it, your shoulders go back where they were. The reason is that posture isn’t a shape to hold, it’s the residue of how your movement starts — and movement almost always starts from the same side. To improve posture so that it stays, the work isn’t straightening the shape: it’s changing where the gesture is born.

If you’ve searched how to improve your posture, it has probably already happened: someone told you to pull your shoulders back, you did a few weeks of exercises, for a while you felt better. Then everything went back to how it was. It isn’t your fault and it isn’t the exercises’ fault: it’s the way the problem is framed.

Why does posture go back as soon as you stop thinking about it?

Because correcting from the outside means holding a shape, and holding costs attention. Attention is limited: the moment you drive, answer a message or get tired, you let it go. And when you do, the body doesn’t return to a wrong shape out of spite: it returns to the one that costs it least.

There’s a second reason, less obvious. In the meantime nothing has changed in the movement that produced that posture. You still climb stairs starting with the same foot, carry the bag on the same shoulder, always turn to the same side. Thousands of repetitions a day on one side, against twenty minutes of exercise three times a week: it isn’t a fair contest.

What is posture, really?

It isn’t a position. It’s what remains when you’re not thinking about anything: the sum of all the gestures you’ve made, always repeated the same way. Posture isn’t the cause of your discomfort, it’s its visible result.

And there’s a detail that explains why the discomfort always returns to the same spot: the body doesn’t move isolated muscles, it moves chains. When you lift a bag the shoulder isn’t working: a line is, starting from the ground, crossing the pelvis and reaching the hand. If that line always switches on from the same side, over the years one side becomes more skilled and more loaded, and the other learns to keep quiet. The shoulder that pulls is often the last link of a chain that begins at a foot.

How to improve your posture, concretely

The work is reversed: instead of correcting the final shape, you act on where the movement begins. It’s three steps, and they go in this order.

1. Notice where the gesture starts

Before changing anything you have to notice what you do. For a week, just observe: which foot goes first up the stairs, which shoulder the bag always ends up on, which leg carries the weight when you stand waiting. Correct nothing. It seems little and it’s the part that changes most, because it moves an automatism into the field of what you can see.

2. Remove before you add

Much of the tension pulling at your shoulders serves no purpose: it stayed switched on with no reason left. Adding exercises on top of an already contracted body means building on something that pulls. First you let go of what’s excess, then you build — and not the other way round.

3. Change the starting point, not the shape

Here’s the real difference. You don’t straighten yourself: you start the same gesture from the other side. Climb the stairs beginning with the foot that usually finishes, carry the bag on the silent shoulder, turn the other way. At first it’s clumsy, and that’s fine: clumsiness is the sign that you’re doing something genuinely new, not that you’re doing it wrong.

The difference from classic postural training isn’t that attention becomes unnecessary here: it’s where you put it. There you hold a final shape; here you are present in the instant the movement begins. And that instant is precise: when weight passes from one leg to the other there is a moment when neither is in charge yet, and it’s there — only there — that choice is possible. If you’re not present in that moment, the gesture restarts on its own from the usual side.

Beware a promise you’ll often hear, and that I won’t make: it isn’t true that after a while it happens by itself. What changes without having to be remembered is availability: the chain learns to transmit, the rebound fades, the silent side stops being mute. But choosing remains an act, and an act is performed every time. Anyone guaranteeing it will become automatic is promising you a second automatism in place of the first — and you came here to get out of one.

An exercise to do right now, in two minutes

Standing, slowly shift your weight onto one leg, as if you wanted to lift the other. Do it slowly. If the starting leg lets you go, the weight slides onto the other and stays there. If it doesn’t, at some point you feel a small tug pulling you back, like a stretched elastic snapping.

That tug — the rebound — is the information you were looking for: it says a chain hasn’t been set down, that this side won’t let you leave. Repeat it on both sides and compare. Almost always one side is clean and the other isn’t, and that’s where the work begins.

So is postural training useless?

I didn’t say that. It works, and in some cases it’s exactly what’s needed: after an injury, when a muscle is genuinely weak, or when someone needs to regain confidence with their body under professional guidance. Its limit isn’t that it’s wrong: it’s that on its own it’s incomplete. It strengthens and lengthens, but it doesn’t touch the question everything comes from — which side movement starts from.

When you need a doctor instead

If you have acute pain, pain that wakes you at night, tingling or loss of strength, this article isn’t for you: the right place is your doctor, and then possibly a physiotherapist. What I’m talking about here is movement education, not a clinical intervention, and it replaces neither. It deals with how you use your body, not with treating it.

Where to start

The best gesture to start from is the one you do most often and think about least: walking.
