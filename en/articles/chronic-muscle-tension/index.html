Chronic muscle tension: why the same side is always more tense

Jacopo Fedeli · 10 min read

In short

Chronic muscle tension is a contraction that no longer switches off once the movement is over. Almost always it settles on the same side of the body, because that side has become the specialist that holds, pulls and grips for both. Stretching and massage give relief, but if you don’t change who does the work the tension returns: releasing it at the root means giving the task back to the other side.

Touch the shoulder that always bothers you. You know it already, don’t you? You know which side it’s on before you even look. It’s almost always the same one: chronic tension has a favourite side. And that, more than a detail, is the key to understanding it — and to letting it go.

An honest premise: this is educational work, not therapy. If you have persistent or severe pain, or pain that wakes you at night, the first thing to do is get assessed by a doctor or physiotherapist. What you read here helps you understand how tension organizes itself; it doesn’t replace a diagnosis.

The definition

What chronic muscle tension is

Chronic muscle tension is a contraction that stays on even when it’s no longer needed. A muscle, after doing its job, should go soft again. When instead it stays in grip for hours, days, years, that tension has stopped following a function: it keeps pulling with no reason left.

In Bilateral Education we call it orphan tone — tension orphaned of the gesture that once called it. It isn’t muscle damage, it isn’t something to repair: it’s a habit of contraction the body took on and never set down.

Feel. Lift your shoulders toward your ears, hold for five seconds, then let them drop. That small soft collapse — that’s what a healthy muscle does when it’s finished. Chronic tension is a muscle that has forgotten how to make that drop.

The point

Why it always settles on the same side

Chronic tension isn’t spread at random: it settles where the body always works the same way.

You always carry the bag on the same shoulder. You always hold the phone with the same hand. You always turn toward the screen on the same side. Year after year, one side becomes the specialist that holds and grips for both — and a specialist that never lets go ends up in permanent grip. The other side, meanwhile, does less and less: as we describe when talking about motor coordination, it isn’t weaker, it’s just disconnected.

So chronic tension is, almost always, the symptom of an imbalance: one side carrying the load of two. That’s why acting only on that side — massaging it, stretching it, warming it — relieves but doesn’t resolve. You’re treating the one that works too much, without ever calling in the one that works too little.

Tension or pain

Tension or pain? When tension becomes chronic

They’re two different things that often travel together. Tension is a state of the muscle: it’s in grip, hard to the touch, pulling. Pain is a signal: it can arise from that tension, but not always, and not only from there.

Tension is called chronic when it no longer discharges on its own — when it stays even after rest, after sleep, after the apparent cause has passed. It’s the moment the contraction stops being a response and becomes a set-up: the body is like that, by default.

If in place of tension there’s real, persistent pain, go back to the premise: that’s a professional’s ground. Here we’re talking about the tension that isn’t (yet) illness — the kind you live with and take for granted.

Why relief doesn’t last

Why stretching and massage only work for a while

Because they touch the effect, not the cause. A massage or a good stretch momentarily releases the muscle in grip: for a few hours you breathe easier. Then you go back to your life — same bag, same shoulder, same side holding everything — and the tension reforms, because the reason it was there hasn’t changed.

Listen. Have you ever said «I just did physiotherapy and two days later I’m back to square one»? It’s not that physiotherapy doesn’t work: it’s that tension isn’t a knot to untie once, it’s a behaviour of the body — and behaviours return until you change what produces them.

How it releases

How to release chronic muscle tension

Here Bilateral Education proposes a reversal. To release doesn’t mean to «relax» — an order we give the muscle that almost never works. It means to make the tension migrate: to move it from where it stayed stuck toward movement, until it becomes a loan again and not a property. The Method does it in three phases.

First you remove. With slow release exercises — they come from Yang-style Taiji — you don’t «try to relax»: you go so slowly that the tension, with no task left to cling to, begins to let go on its own. Slowness exposes the automatism; softness makes it perceptible.

Then you build — from the other side. Here’s the move all the other approaches miss: you give work back to the disconnected side, on the same task the strong one does alone today. It’s not generic exercise: it’s teaching the still side to carry its share, so the side in grip can finally let go. Tension leaves when it stops being necessary.

Finally you integrate. A few still minutes, attention on the breath, so the new balance can settle. It’s the only part you can redo on your own, five minutes a day.

The most common case

Neck and shoulders: the most common case

It’s no accident that chronic tension almost always seeks the neck and trapezius. They’re the muscles that hold: the head, the gaze, the posture in front of the screen. And holding is exactly the task one side tends to take on alone.

Watch someone at a computer: one shoulder is almost always a little higher than the other, the head slightly turned to one side. That micro-asymmetry, held eight hours a day, is the factory of neck tension. Releasing it by working only on the neck is like drying the floor without closing the tap: the tap is the imbalanced set-up of the whole body.

Try it

How to tell which side you’re imbalanced on

Two tests, thirty seconds.

Experiment · 30 seconds
  1. Standing, eyes closed. On which foot do you feel more weight? The body has already chosen, and it tells you at once.
  2. With your fingers, press along the edge of the neck and over both shoulders, first one side then the other. One side is harder, more «taut cord». That’s your holding side.

If you want a fuller map of how you distribute your body, the side test is made for it: eight questions that don’t measure how good you are, but how much you know of what you do every day without noticing.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

What’s the difference between muscle tension and a muscle cramp?
A cramp is a sudden, sharp shortening of a muscle, often painful and tied to a specific effort. Chronic muscle tension is quieter and more prolonged: a constant, low-level grip that lasts over time and that you usually can’t trace to a single episode.

Can chronic muscle tension cause back pain?
Yes, it can contribute. A long-held imbalance — one side holding more than the other — loads the spine and its supporting muscles asymmetrically. But chronic back pain has many possible causes: if it persists, it should always be assessed by a professional, not managed alone.

How do you release neck tension?
In the moment, heat, slow movement and massage help. But if it keeps coming back, the neck isn’t the real problem: the imbalanced set-up that forces it to hold is. Lasting relief comes from rebalancing the two sides, not from treating the neck alone.

Do supplements like magnesium help?
Magnesium can help a fatigued or cramp-prone muscle, and it’s reasonable in some cases. But it doesn’t touch the tension that has stopped following a function: that isn’t a deficiency, it’s a habit of contraction. No supplement changes who does the work in your body.

How long does it take to release chronic tension?
Immediate relief can come in a single session. But for it not to return takes time, because you’re changing a motor habit years long: weeks of consistent practice, not a one-off intervention.

In short

In short

Chronic muscle tension isn’t a fault to repair: it’s a body that has learned to always hold from the same side. You can ease it each time with a massage, and that’s fine — but if you want it gone, the road is another: give work back to the side you’ve left behind, so the tired side can finally let go.

Start with the Test to see which side you’re imbalanced on, then look at the Method to understand how you remove before you build. One body, two sides — and the tension that releases when you stop asking one side alone to do all the work.
