The session

Ninety minutes on your gesture.

The session works on your movement: the gesture you repeat every day, or the one from your sport. Alone, or in pairs.

Let’s Talk

What happens in ninety minutes

The shape is always this, and the order isn’t negotiable. What changes every time is the central part: that’s where we work on your gesture, and it changes with you.

00 – 20 min

Remove

A few release exercises, standing, to let go of the tension that keeps one side glued. They come from Taiji, from a school I have practised for over ten years, reread for one purpose only: to free the movement.

20 – 65 min

Your gesture

This is the heart, and it is different every time: it changes with your sport, your work, what you feel that day. We observe where the gesture starts and try starting it from the other side. Immersive, not repetitive.

65 – 90 min

Integrate

Still, with attention on the whole body. It is the part that seems least useful and it is the one that makes everything else stay — and the only one you can redo alone, five minutes a day.

Where

Where you train

For one-to-one sessions I come to the place where you already train: the gym, the studio, the space where you do your sport. It’s the better option if the gesture you want to work on is tied to a specific piece of equipment or space.

What changes

It isn’t a cure. But something changes, and it shows.

Straight away

You notice things you’ve been doing for twenty years

Which foot starts, which shoulder carries, which way you turn. It seems little: it’s the shift from automatism to something observable, and it happens the very first time. In the same session you feel the tension your body was dragging for no reason let go. And you take home a test you can redo alone in twenty seconds.

With practice

The load stops going to the same place

A body that always starts from the same side accumulates it there. Being able to start from the other one distributes it, and over twenty years that’s the difference between a joint that wears out and one that takes turns resting. Meanwhile the weight reaches the ground more fully: stable without stiffening.

Where it shows

Not stronger on one side: available on both

It doesn’t make you better at the gesture you already have: it gives you the one you don’t. Fighters stop being readable. Performers no longer have their presence tilting to the same side. And anyone, when the usual side is tired, has an alternative instead of stopping. When the strong one can’t, the other is no longer a stranger.

Everything else rests on a single instant. When you shift your weight and feel that tug, the body is still, in a tension that isn’t movement yet — and that’s where there are three ways out: go with it, bounce back, or open a new direction. Learning doesn’t mean starting from the other side: it means recognising that instant and staying inside it instead of rushing through.

What it isn’t

It isn’t a massage and isn’t a treatment: I don’t fix you, you learn. It isn’t physiotherapy and doesn’t replace your doctor: if you have acute pain, tingling or loss of strength, your doctor comes first.

Nor is it a workout: no sweating, no counting reps, no need to be fit. And it doesn’t touch the technique of your sport, which stays your coach’s job: here we work on the point technique starts from.

Who it works for, and who it doesn’t

It works for people who have lived for years with one side in charge and want to regain use of the other: athletes, performers, people who bring their body into their work, and anyone with a shoulder or hip that always complains in the same spot.

It doesn’t work if you’re after an instant result with no practice, or if you expect someone else to fix you. Here you learn to notice and to choose: it takes attention, and some clumsiness at the start.
