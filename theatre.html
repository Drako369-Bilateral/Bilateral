The tension

Your body has a default — and the audience feels it

Most performers unconsciously lead with one side. Weight settles, gestures reach, and focus turns along a habitual diagonal. On stage this reads as a limited palette: the same shapes, the same balance, the same line of intention — repeated without choice.

The approach

Not more choreography — less restriction

Bilateral doesn't add a catalogue of mirrored moves. It works subtractively: releasing the chronic co-contraction and habitual dominance that keep one side in charge, until both sides become equally available.

Grounded in Merleau-Ponty's body schema and in release work drawn from Yang-style Taiji, the aim is not symmetry for its own sake but freedom — the ability to choose which side leads, moment to moment.

What you'll leave with

A wider physical vocabulary

Expanded gestural range

Movement vocabulary that isn't locked to one dominant side.

Balanced presence

Weight and focus that read as centred, not skewed.

Elective leading side

Choose your lead consciously, instead of defaulting to it.

Finer body awareness

A sharper sense of habitual tension and how to let it go.
